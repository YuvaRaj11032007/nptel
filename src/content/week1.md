## Summary

Week 1 serves as a foundational overview of Computational Genomics and the "omics" sciences. It traces the history of genomics from early sequencing milestones (Fred Sanger, the Human Genome Project) to modern high-throughput capabilities generating massive "Big Data". It outlines the core central dogma (DNA -> RNA -> Protein) and establishes how this translates to corresponding omics fields (Genomics -> Transcriptomics -> Proteomics), alongside Metagenomics and Epigenomics.

The lectures also compare the structural organization of prokaryotic and eukaryotic genomes, highlighting the complexity introduced by introns, exons, jumping genes (transposons), and repeat elements (SINEs/LINEs). Finally, it walks through key bioinformatics databases that catalog this exponentially growing biological data, covering primary repositories like NCBI, EMBL, DDBJ, structural databases like PDB, and pathway/metabolic databases like KEGG and MetaCyc. 

## Entities
* **Fred Sanger**: Developed automated DNA sequencing, enabling early genome sequencing.
* **J. Craig Venter**: Led the team at TIGR that sequenced the first bacterial genome (*H. influenzae*).
* **NCBI (National Center for Biotechnology Information)**: Major repository for molecular biology information.
* **EBI (European Bioinformatics Institute)**: European counterpart for biological data, part of INSDC.
* **DDBJ (DNA Data Bank of Japan)**: Japanese counterpart, part of INSDC.

## Concepts
* **Computational Biology & Bioinformatics**: The analysis of biological data using computers, crucial given the rise of high-throughput technologies.
* **The "Omics" Domains**: 
  * **Genomics**: Studying the complete set of DNA.
  * **Transcriptomics**: Studying RNA molecules actively expressed.
  * **Proteomics**: Studying the full repertoire of proteins and their modifications.
  * **Metagenomics**: Sequencing DNA directly from environmental/microbial communities without laboratory culturing.
* **Genome Organization**:
  * Prokaryotes usually feature small genomes, high gene density, operons, and lack introns.
  * Eukaryotes are complex, governed by the "C-value enigma" (genome size doesn't strictly correlate with complexity), containing massive non-coding sequences like LINEs, SINEs, and pseudogenes.
* **Databases & Data Search**: The importance of DBMS in biological sciences. Examples include SWISS-PROT (curated proteins), PDB (3D structures), SRA (sequence reads), and KEGG/MetaCyc (metabolic pathways).
